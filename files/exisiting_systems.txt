EXISTING SYSTEMS (FROM PREVIOUS PAPERS ONLY)

Overview
The reviewed papers describe deepfake detection systems that rely on deep learning
for spatial artifact detection, temporal inconsistency modeling, and sometimes
frequency-domain cues. Surveys emphasize that while these systems can achieve strong
performance on benchmark datasets, generalisation to unseen manipulations remains a
major limitation, and dataset diversity is critical for robust deployment. [1,2,4,5]

1) Spatial CNN-Based Detectors
- XceptionNet: A CNN architecture widely used for deepfake detection on datasets like
  FaceForensics++. It achieves high accuracy in benchmark evaluations and is frequently
  reported as a top-performing baseline. [4]
- MesoNet (Meso-4 and MesoInception-4): Lightweight CNN architectures designed to detect
  mesoscopic artifacts in manipulated faces, offering competitive accuracy with fewer
  parameters. [4]
- Transfer learning CNNs for image deepfakes: CNN-based classification of real vs fake
  images with preprocessing, dataset splitting, and hyperparameter tuning are reported
  in image-focused studies. [3]

2) Temporal and Spatio-Temporal Systems
- CNN + LSTM (Conv-LSTM): Uses CNNs for frame-level feature extraction and LSTM for
  sequence modeling to detect temporal inconsistencies in video clips. [4]
- Recurrent convolutional strategies: Recurrent convolutional networks use face-aligned
  frame sequences to model temporal inconsistencies across frames. [4]
- Spatial-temporal video transformers: Transformer-based models capture spatial artifacts
  and temporal inconsistencies with self-attention mechanisms for video-level detection. [4]

3) Artifact-Driven and Hybrid Systems
- Face warping artifacts: Detects resolution and affine warping artifacts introduced
  during face synthesis, using CNNs trained on simulated warping inconsistencies. [4]
- Two-stream neural network: Combines a face classification stream (high-level artifacts)
  and a patch-triplet stream (low-level noise residuals) for tampered face detection. [4]
- Visual artifacts (eyes, teeth, contours): Classical visual cues are used to detect
  deepfake artifacts with lightweight classifiers. [4]

4) Capsule Networks
- Capsule-Forensics: Uses capsule networks to detect fake images and videos, with
  variants for different input sizes and dropout/noise regularisation. [4]

5) Systematic Review Insights on Detection Families
- Visual deepfake detection techniques are grouped into spatial, temporal, frequency,
  and spatio-temporal categories. These categories guide system design and evaluation
  across datasets and manipulation types. [1]
- Surveys emphasize dataset quality and diversity as critical to detection performance,
  and note that many systems overfit to popular datasets, limiting generalisation. [2,5]
